## Framework: The Slippery Slide Effect

The slide metaphor: once the reader starts, they cannot stop. Each element pulls to the next:

```
Intriguing headline
    → Lead that expands the intrigue
        → First simple sentence that leads to the second
            → Paragraph that ends with a hook
                → Next paragraph that delivers and creates a new hook
                    → ... all the way to the CTA
```

**Golden rule of the slide:** the end of every paragraph must make the reader NEED to read the next one. Never close a paragraph on a resting point — close it on a tension point.

## The 4 Curiosity Tools

### 1. Open Loop (Unresolved Gap)
Open a question or promise that will only be closed further ahead. The reader cannot stop because the brain hates unresolved gaps.

**How to create:**
- Mention you will reveal something at the end: "Before I tell you the secret, I need to show you why 97% of people get this wrong..."
- Ask a question and delay the answer: "Why does this method work even when everything else has failed? The answer will surprise you — but first..."
- Number what is coming: "There are 3 reasons this happens. The third one will shock you."

### 2. Information Gap
Create the perception that the reader does not know something they need to know. The discomfort of not knowing is the engine of curiosity.

**Structures that work:**
> "What you should never say to an auditor — and what to say instead"
> "Why almost everyone gets this wrong — including most experts"
> "The one place you should NEVER store your gold"

### 3. Curiosity Seed
Short phrases inserted strategically that promise something even better ahead.

**Direct examples:**
> "But I didn't stop there."
> "But the most surprising part comes next."
> "And then something happened that I didn't expect."
> "Keep reading — the best part is still coming."
> "Now comes the part nobody talks about."

### 4. Intriguing Specificity
Ultra-specific details that make the reader ask "how?" or "why?".

> "Why do the French live longer even eating like kings and smoking like chimneys?" (makes you ask: how is that possible?)
> "The towel-hanging trick that increases strength by 40%" (makes you ask: what trick is that?)
> "How to get rid of 127 types of weeds with a can of diet Coke in 48 hours" (makes you ask: how?)

---

## Curiosity in Headlines vs. Body Text

**In headlines:** create the gap without closing it. The reader must NEED to read to get the answer.
> "What you should never eat on a plane" (does not reveal what)
> "Why some posts explode on Instagram — and others disappear in seconds"

**In body text:** open and partially close, creating new gaps. Every answer generates a new question.

**Transitions that keep the slide going:**
- "But that's only half the story..."
- "And here's where things get interesting..."
- "What I discovered next changed everything..."
- "But wait — there's more..."
- "And this is where most people make the fatal mistake..."

---

## How to Create Mystery Without Frustrating

Curiosity without delivery destroys trust. Rules to avoid frustrating:

1. **Always deliver what you promised**: if you promised to reveal the secret, reveal it — and make it worth it
2. **The delivery must exceed the expectation**: the answer must be at least as good as the reader imagined
3. **Don't delay too long**: gaps that are too long make the reader give up. Curiosity seeds should be resolved within 3 to 5 paragraphs at most
4. **Deliver real value, not just surprise**: curiosity that leads to a genuine insight builds trust; curiosity that leads to a cheap trick destroys it

---

## Fatal Mistakes

1. **Closing paragraphs on a resting point**: "...and that's why it works." lets the reader leave. "...and that's why it works — but there's one detail almost everyone ignores." keeps them
2. **Promising and not delivering**: a gap that never closes is a broken promise. Once, the reader forgives. Twice, they quit
3. **Vague specificity**: "an amazing secret" creates no curiosity. "the secret a Boston doctor discovered while treating Alzheimer's patients" does
4. **Curiosity without relevance**: intriguing the reader with something that doesn't matter to them wastes attention and breaks credibility
5. **Too many open loops at once**: more than 2 open gaps simultaneously overwhelms the reader. Close one before opening another
