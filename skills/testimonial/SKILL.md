## Framework: The 5 Criteria of an Elite Testimonial

| Criterion | Description | Bad Example | Good Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Specificity** | Number, timeframe, concrete result | "I loved the product" | "I lost 18 lbs in 6 weeks" |
| **Attribution** | Full name, role, city, company | "J.S." | "John Smith, accountant, Chicago" |
| **Relevance** | Attacks the audience's main objection | Talks about another benefit | Talks exactly about what the audience doubts |
| **Authenticity** | Sounds like a real person, not copy | Perfect, edited language | Has natural speech imperfections |
| **Photo/Proof** | Visual element that confirms the person | No image | Real photo of the customer |

## The 5 Types of Testimonials

### 1. Specific Result
The most powerful. Shows the "before and after" in numbers.
> "I had $1,847 in my account and didn't know how to pay rent. In 4 months following the method, I earned $23,000 in a single month. I didn't believe it until I saw the bank statement." — Peter Allen, 41, Los Angeles

### 2. Personal Story
Short narrative that creates identification. Ideal for leads and openings.
> "I tried 6 different diets over 3 years. I'd lose weight, gain it back, and feel embarrassed. With this protocol, for the first time in my life, I'm at the weight I wanted — and I don't feel like I'm depriving myself of anything." — Anna Brooks, teacher, Portland

### 3. Expert Validation
Uses a professional's authority to validate the product with their technical expertise.
> "In the last 8 months, I've learned more from Dr. Sjuggerud about the wide world of investing than I learned in 19 years as a broker. The recommendations are conservative, make sense, and generate profits consistently." — Andrew Lawson, stockbroker, Charleston, SC

### 4. Comparison with Alternatives
Destroys the "I've tried other things" objection by showing specific superiority.
> "I used 4 scheduling tools before this one. None of them had [feature X]. Now I save 3 hours a week that I used to spend on manual tasks." — Carlos Mendes, marketing agency

### 5. Track Record with Data
For financial products, investments, or accumulated results.
> "Average of 53.07% profit per trade. If you had invested $5,000 in each recommendation, you could have more than $71,000 in profit alone over the last 12 months."

## The 5 Questions to Collect Powerful Testimonials

Send these questions to satisfied customers by email or form:

1. **What was your biggest problem or frustration BEFORE using [product]?** (the "before" pain)
2. **What did you specifically achieve AFTER using [product]?** (result with number and timeframe)
3. **What was the moment when you realized it was working?** (creates narrative and specificity)
4. **What would you say to someone who is unsure whether or not to buy?** (destroys third-party objections)
5. **May I use your name, photo, and city in this testimonial?** (permission request)

## Where to Position in Copy

| Position | Type of Testimonial | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **Teaser / envelope** | The most impactful one you have | Attracts the click or the open |
| **Right after the lead** | Personal story | Confirms the promise is real |
| **After the product presentation** | Specific result | Proves it works |
| **Before the CTA** | Comparison or track record | Removes the final objection before purchase |
| **Full sales page** | Interspersed throughout the copy | Sustains credibility from start to finish |

## How to Format for Maximum Impact

**Standard structure:**
```
"[Specific result in 1 sentence — the most impactful first]

[Before context — 1 to 2 sentences]

[What changed and how — 2 to 3 sentences with details]

[Final recommendation — 1 direct sentence]"

— [Full Name], [role or profession], [city/state]
```

**Formatting tips:**
- Use large quotes and visual emphasis to separate from the body text
- Bold the most powerful sentence (the specific result)
- Include a real photo whenever possible
- Do not correct the customer's grammar — imperfections increase authenticity

---

## Fatal Mistakes

1. **Generic testimonial**: "Amazing product, highly recommend!" has no conversion power
2. **No full attribution**: "M.S." looks made up. Full name + city = credibility
3. **Stock photo**: the reader recognizes it and immediately discredits the rest
4. **Testimonial irrelevant to the objection**: if the audience fears the price, the testimonial must address cost-benefit
5. **Over-editing**: testimonials that look written by a copywriter lose authenticity
6. **Fabricating or exaggerating**: besides being unethical, it destroys all credibility when discovered
